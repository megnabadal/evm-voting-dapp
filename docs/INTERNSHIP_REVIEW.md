# Internship Review

A self-review reflecting on my 30-day software development internship — both the work and the experience.

---

## What I'm Proud Of

This internship gave me a genuinely strong overview of fullstack development and software building. I came in with limited exposure to most of the stack and left with hands-on experience across every layer — frontend, backend, database, smart contracts, deployment, and Web3 integration. The breadth was the value. I didn't just touch each area; I built something working in each one, and I understand how they connect.

The biggest single learning win was blockchain. Solidity, EVM concepts, wallet authentication, gas economics, on-chain events — these were entirely new to me at the start, and by the end of 30 days I was comfortable enough to debug an event signature mismatch by reading Etherscan and reasoning about contract bytecode versus my local source. That kind of competence didn't exist on day one.

Beyond the technical learning, the internship gave me practical exposure to how production software actually gets built: Git workflows that survive real branching strategies, deployment platforms (Vercel, Railway, Neon, Alchemy) that I'd only read about, the discipline of writing documentation while the work is fresh instead of trying to reconstruct it later, and the experience of debugging across the full stack when something breaks.

---

## What Could Have Gone Differently

Honestly, very little. The internship itself was well-structured — the daily plan, weekly milestones, and final sprint cadence all worked. The mentor was responsive when I had questions, the scope was ambitious but realistic, and the project was a meaningful integration of everything we were supposed to learn rather than disconnected toy exercises.

The one external constraint was time. The 30-day window was set by my college academic calendar, not by the company, and there were moments where I would have liked an extra week to go deeper on certain things — particularly smart contract testing, security considerations, and gas optimization. That's not a critique of the internship structure; it's just an honest observation that I could have absorbed more if I'd had more runway.

If I'd had two more weeks, I would have used them on:
- Writing proper Hardhat tests for the smart contract instead of relying on manual verification
- Exploring Foundry as an alternative testing toolchain
- Implementing token-weighted voting to understand a more realistic governance pattern
- Adding error tracking and monitoring to the deployed services

None of these were blockers for completing the scoped work. They were directions I would have liked to explore.

---

## Internship Structure Feedback

What worked particularly well:

- **The "build a real thing" approach.** Spending the entire internship on a single integrated project, rather than disconnected exercises, made every learning compound. By week three I was making architectural decisions that drew on knowledge from week one.

- **Daily commits as a discipline.** Forcing daily progress visible in Git history kept momentum up and made progress legible — both to me and to the mentor.

- **Weekend learning days.** Building in time specifically for reading and absorbing concepts (rather than only producing) was useful. By the time I started using a new library, I usually had at least surface-level understanding of why it existed.

- **The final sprint structure.** The Kanban board for the closeout week turned "polish and ship" into discrete, completable tasks. Without that structure, the documentation and demo work could easily have been rushed or skipped.

- **Mentor availability.** Being able to ask questions and get responses without waiting days made the difference between hours of confused debugging and quick clarifications.

What would have been even better with more time:

- **More pair programming or code review.** I wrote almost all the code solo. More opportunities to have senior eyes on what I produced — and to see how a more experienced developer would have approached the same problem — would have accelerated learning further.

- **Earlier exposure to testing patterns.** I deferred testing too long because the educational scope front-loaded learning concepts and back-loaded discipline practices. A test-first approach introduced earlier might have stuck better.

---

## My Performance Self-Assessment

Where I think I did well:

- **Showed up consistently.** Daily commits, daily progress, no significant gaps in productivity across the 30 days.
- **Asked questions promptly when stuck**, rather than spinning on problems for days.
- **Took feedback seriously** and applied it without ego, including when it required redoing work.
- **Documented as I went**, which paid off massively at the end.
- **Pushed for understanding over copy-paste.** When I used a pattern, I tried to understand why it worked, not just that it worked.

Where I'd score myself harder:

- **Testing discipline was weak.** I know test-first development is the right pattern, but I didn't internalize it as habit during this project. Most of my testing was manual.
- **Some integration gaps between frontend and backend** that I attribute to not defining the API contract tightly enough up front. Better planning would have saved real debugging time later.
- **Comfort with Git was uneven.** I could handle the basic workflow, but more complex scenarios (the docs-only branches, conflicting merges, rebasing) required more help than they should have. That's a fluency gap I'll keep working on.

---

## What I'm Taking Forward

After this internship, I'm leaving with:

- A working portfolio piece I can demonstrate end-to-end
- Real working knowledge of every layer of a modern Web3 application
- A documented set of lessons learned that I can apply to the next project
- Confidence that I can pick up new tools and frameworks and ship working software with them
- A clearer picture of what kind of work I want to do next — closer to the smart contract layer than I expected before starting

I'd describe the internship as a genuine accelerator. The 30 days delivered more practical exposure than the equivalent time in academic coursework would have, and I'm grateful for the structure, the mentorship, and the scope of what I was trusted to build.

---

## Closing

Would I recommend this internship to another student? Yes, without hesitation. The structure works. The project is well-chosen. The mentorship is responsive. The technical scope is ambitious enough to stretch you without being so unrealistic that you give up. And the deliverable at the end is something you can point at and say "I built that" with full ownership.

The only thing I'd tell future interns is to clear their schedule for the 30 days as completely as possible. The internship rewards consistent daily effort, and I think I would have learned even more if my time hadn't been split with college obligations.

— Megna Badal
